Urban environmental inequality in the 19th century and the toxic legacy of the manufactured gas industry: Unearthing early fossil fuel infrastructure in American cities

Jonathan Tollefson, Scott Frickel

Brown University

Problem

Systematically addressing the historical dimensions of environmental inequality has remained a distinct methodological and, therefore, theoretical challenge. This is due in part to a conspicuous lack of comprehensive historical site data: Contemporary spatial environmental inequality research rests on the availability of geolocated site data maintained by state and federal regulatory agencies, but little such data are available prior to midcentury.

We also can't extrapolate theories of urban change and environmental risk backward, because the social-spatial organization of cities was significantly different a hundred years ago. Recent research into the historical dynamics of urban residential patterns has demonstrated the scalar quality of urban segregation over time – from street-level segregation at the beginning of the 1900s to neighborhood-level effects by mid-century (Logan et al 2020) – underscoring the need for a detailed accounting of historical environmental inequality at precise spatial scales. Yet recovering data on historic urban environmental hazards is a significant challenge.

Our solution

To model environmental inequality in the 19th century, we outline a new, scalable approach to identifying a particular legacy industrial hazard – infrastructure associated with the manufactured gas industry of the late-19th to early-20th century – using a computational analysis of historic map data. A pilot study focused on Providence, RI, demonstrates its utility.

First, we identify multiple significant potentially-hazardous sites unknown to state or federal regulators; second, a proximity analysis of newly-identified sites using historic census data demonstrates the necessity of a street-level approach to measuring environmental inequality across time. Our goal is twofold: To validate the utility of our approach to site identification – as a tool to efficiently identify a class of industrial sites that represent a distinct source of historic environment risk as well as a continued hazard to current residents of US cities – and to demonstrate a method to assess historical environmental inequality at a fine-grained spatial scale.

Background: Manuf. gas industry

Manufactured gas was the first city-wide fossil fuel utility. From about 1860 to 1950, most homes, institutions, and factories in the US and much of Europe relied on gas produced from coal for lighting, heat, and cooking. Big cities might have dozens of gas production and distribution sites, and even smaller cities would have had at least one manufactured gas plant (or MGP) by the end of the 1800s. MGPs were a defining feature of the built environment of cities from that period - especially the enormous gas storage tanks, or “gasometers": Enormous cylindrical structures, made of brick and iron, up to hundreds of feet in diameter and several stories (see below).

 

The manufactured gas industry produced over 15 trillion cubic feet of gas - as well as over 11 billion gallons of toxic waste (Radian Corporation 1985). The number of contaminated gas sites has been estimated at somewhere between 30,000 and 50,000 in the US alone (Hatheway 2012). Only a small fraction of these sites have been identified and characterized for remediation.

In addition to posing significant health risks to MGP workers and nearby residents a century ago, the toxic legacy of coal tar waste presents challenging scenarios for waste site characterization and remediation today (Thomas and Lester 1994) and has long been recognized as an important source of environmental, economic, and health risk (Harkins et al. 1987; Phillips 1991).

New methods to identify MGP sites

The first challenge is to recover as many lost gas sites as possible from the historical record.

To identify sites in bulk, we draw on historic insurance maps produced by the Sanborn Map Company, which provide a relatively complete set of building-level map information for most US cities from the late-19th century onward. Sanborn map publications cover every major and minor city in the US, and their publication cycle corresponds with the peak and decline of the manufactured gas industry, making Sanborn maps an ideal source to locate MGP sites in bulk.

We developed an computational pipeline ("Detect_MGP") to efficiently identify MGP sites in historic Sanborn maps by extracting and classifying circular features that correspond to gasometer structures (see below).

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

We trained our pipeline on 16 thousand Sanborn map pages. Our pipeline reduces the time required to locate MGPs in historic Sanborn maps by over 90%, while maintaining a recall rate of nearly 90% as well.

Our pipeline identified 19 sites in the greater Providence area. Only three of those sites have their MGP history listed in state and federal environmental risk databases. See below for identified sites alongside contemporary flood risk data.

Results: Environmental inquality in 1880 and 1930

We measure historical environmental inequality using two approaches, focusing on the twelve sites located in central Providence. A "unit-hazard coincidence" approach counts all individual who live in a neighborhood that hosts at least one MGP site as "at risk." This approach is common in contemporary environmental inequality research, because most demographic measures are only available at the level of individual census tracts. We use census enumeration districts (ED), normalized to 1930 boundaries, as our unit of analysis.

Our second approach calculates risk at the scale of individual street segments, which more closely tracks the scale of urban segregation in the late-19th century (Logan et al 2020).

Coefficients represent the odds ratio for living in the same ED or on the same street as one of the identified MGP sites. (The coefficient for Black individuals in 1930 at the street level is 2.39; this means that Black residents have 2.39 times the odds of living on the same street as an MGP in 1930 compared to equivalent non-Black Providencians).

Discussion

We draw your attention to several patterns. First, race becomes an increasingly important predictor of environmental risk over the study period. Between 1880 and 1930, environmental risk is reorganized: Black and Hispanic residents of Providence become significantly more likley to live near operating or legacy MGP sites. At the same time, first-generation immigrants are relatively "safer" in 1930 compared to the late-19th century.

Second, measuring risk at the street level generally magnifies the impact of race: The difference between Black and Hispanic individuals compared to non-Black and non-Hispanic residents is much greater when measured at the street level compared to the ED. This underscores the need to analyze environmental inequality at a spatial scale appropriate to the historic period.

Abstract

From the mid-19th to the mid-20th century, many homes, institutions, and factories in the United States, Canada, Australia and much of Europe relied on gas produced from coal to light lamps and kitchen stoves. The manufactured gas industry sparked a revolution in urban form and function, while at the same time producing vast quantities of hazardous waste with minimal regulation. Today, the remains of former coal gas production, distribution and disposal sites number in the tens of thousands in the United States alone, mostly hidden by decades of urban change and industrial churning. Here, we introduce a new computational method for identifying lost gasworks sites from historical fire insurance maps. A pilot study in Providence, RI combines coal gas site information with historic street-level census data to assess the differential impact of coal gas infrastructure siting on 19th-century city residents. Through this pilot study, we demonstrate the utility of the proposed approach to site identification and introduce the coal gas case as a tool to reassess historical environmental inequality and urban development.
